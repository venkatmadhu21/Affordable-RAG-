Federal affordable housing programs are designed to assist low-income individuals and families in obtaining safe and affordable housing. Rather than being a single initiative, these programs consist of a variety of options, each with its own specific rules and benefits. The primary objective is to keep housing costs manageable for those who might otherwise have difficulty affording a home.

These programs cater to a wide range of people, including families, seniors, and individuals with disabilities. Through rent subsidies, tax credits, or direct housing assistance, they enable millions of Americans to secure stable housing. For those involved in affordable multifamily housing, it is important to understand the broad scope of affordable housing and the different programs available.

Major Federal Affordable Housing Programs
There are several distinct federal affordable housing programs, each with its own features and requirements. Explore each program below to learn what makes it unique and how it impacts your work.


HUD Subsidies (50059 Certification)
HUD subsidies support privately-owned properties by providing rent assistance to eligible residents. The 50059 Certification process determines how much rent a household pays, with HUD covering the difference between the resident’s contribution and the approved rent.

Property staff are responsible for gathering household information, verifying eligibility, and ensuring compliance with HUD rules. Regular recertification is required to maintain assistance.


Public Housing
Public Housing is owned and managed by local or regional housing authorities. These properties offer affordable rents to low-income families, seniors, and people with disabilities.

Staff must follow local authority guidelines for eligibility and rent calculation, and ensure ongoing compliance with both federal and local regulations.


Rural Housing Development
Rural Housing Development programs are managed by the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA). They provide affordable housing options in rural areas, often targeting communities that may have fewer resources.

Eligibility and compliance requirements are specific to USDA guidelines, and staff must be familiar with rural program rules and application processes.


Low Income Housing Tax Credits (LIHTC)
LIHTC is a program administered through the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) that encourages private developers to build or preserve affordable housing. Properties receive tax credits in exchange for offering units at reduced rents to eligible households.

Onsite staff must verify resident eligibility, maintain compliance records, and ensure that income limits are consistently applied.

How Income Limits Affect Eligibility
Income limits are central to affordable housing eligibility. They ensure that assistance goes to those who need it most. Explore the sections below to see how income limits are determined and applied.


How AMI is calculated
Area Median Income (AMI) is calculated by analyzing the incomes of all households in a specific area, then finding the midpoint. This figure is updated annually and used to set income thresholds for affordable housing programs.

Each program uses AMI to define what counts as low, very low, or extremely low income, tailoring eligibility to local conditions.


Examples of AMI in different cities
For instance, the AMI in San Francisco is significantly higher than in Dallas. This means a family earning $80,000 might qualify for assistance in Dallas but not in San Francisco, where the income threshold is higher.

These differences ensure that affordable housing remains accessible in both high- and low-cost areas.


How income limits are used in eligibility decisions
Income limits are set as a percentage of AMI, such as 50% or 80%. Applicants must have household incomes below these limits to qualify for assistance.

Onsite staff use these limits during the application and recertification process to determine if a household is eligible for affordable housing programs.
