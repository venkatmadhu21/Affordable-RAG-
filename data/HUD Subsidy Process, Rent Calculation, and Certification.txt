The HUD subsidy process is designed to make housing affordable for low-income residents by bridging the gap between what a resident can pay and the actual rent. HUD provides funds to property owners, allowing them to offer reduced rents to eligible households.

The process begins with determining a resident’s eligibility, followed by calculating their rent based on income and allowable deductions. Once the resident’s portion is set, HUD pays the remaining amount directly to the property owner, ensuring that the total rent is covered and the resident’s housing remains affordable.

This partnership between HUD, property owners, and residents is crucial for maintaining affordable housing. Property staff play a key role in gathering information, verifying eligibility, and ensuring all steps are completed accurately and on time.

Key Steps in the HUD Subsidy Process
Each step in the HUD subsidy process is important for ensuring compliance and accurate rent calculation. Explore the steps below to understand your responsibilities and how they fit together.

Determining Resident Eligibility
Eligibility is established by collecting detailed information about each household member, including income, assets, and qualifying expenses. Staff must verify this information through documentation and interviews to ensure the household meets HUD’s requirements before move-in and during annual recertifications.


Calculating Resident Rent
Resident rent is calculated based on the household’s annual income, minus allowable deductions such as childcare or medical expenses. The result is the adjusted income, which determines the resident’s portion of the rent. HUD regulations specify the calculation method to ensure fairness and consistency.


Applying the HUD Subsidy
Once the resident’s rent portion is determined, HUD pays the difference between this amount and the contract rent for the unit. This subsidy ensures that residents pay no more than a set percentage of their income, typically around 30%, making housing affordable.


Completing the 50059 Certification
The 50059 Certification is the official documentation of eligibility and rent calculation. Staff must complete and submit this form for each resident, ensuring all information is accurate and up-to-date. The certification process is required at move-in and during annual or interim recertifications.

Income, Assets, and Allowable Deductions
Correctly identifying and verifying income, assets, and allowable deductions is essential for accurate rent calculation and compliance. Explore the sections below for practical examples and tips.

Types of Income (with examples)
Income includes wages, salaries, Social Security, pensions, unemployment benefits, and regular contributions from family or friends. All sources must be reported and verified to ensure the correct rent is calculated.

For example, a resident who receives both a paycheck and Social Security must include both in their annual income calculation.


Types of Assets (with examples)
Assets include checking and savings accounts, stocks, bonds, real estate, and retirement accounts. The value and income generated from these assets are considered in the rent calculation process.

For instance, interest earned from a savings account or dividends from stocks must be included as income from assets.


Allowable Deductions (with examples)
Allowable deductions can include unreimbursed medical expenses for elderly or disabled households, childcare expenses for working families, and certain disability assistance expenses.

For example, if a resident pays for prescription medications or child care, these costs may be deducted from their annual income to lower their rent payment
