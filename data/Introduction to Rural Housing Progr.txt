Rural housing programs are designed to provide safe, affordable housing options for individuals and families living in rural areas. These programs often use low-interest loans, grants, and rental assistance to make housing accessible to those who might otherwise struggle to find suitable accommodations. Both the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) and the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) play significant roles in administering these programs, ensuring that a range of housing needs are met across rural America.

The scope of rural housing programs includes developing, rehabilitating, and preserving rental properties for low-income families, the elderly, persons with disabilities, and farmworkers. By supporting property owners and developers, these programs help maintain the availability of affordable housing and contribute to the well-being of rural communities.

The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) supports rural housing primarily through programs that fund affordable rental housing and provide rental assistance. HUD’s initiatives often focus on ensuring that low-income individuals and families in rural areas have access to safe, decent, and affordable housing options.

HUD collaborates with local agencies and property owners to administer rental assistance and support the development of housing for families, the elderly, and persons with disabilities. While HUD’s reach is national, its rural housing efforts are tailored to address the unique challenges faced by rural communities.

Who Benefits from Rural Housing?
Rural housing programs are designed to serve specific populations with unique needs. Expand each section to learn more about the groups these programs support.

Families
Families in rural areas often face limited housing options and may struggle with affordability. Rural housing programs help low- and moderate-income families access safe, stable homes, supporting their well-being and community involvement.

Eligibility is typically based on income, and families may also receive priority if they need accessible units or other special accommodations.


Elderly
Elderly individuals benefit from housing designed with accessibility and supportive services in mind. These projects often include features like grab bars, ramps, and community spaces to enhance safety and social interaction.

Eligibility usually requires at least one household member to be age 62 or older or to have a qualifying disability.


Individuals with Disabilities
People with physical or mental disabilities are a key focus of rural housing programs. Accessible units and supportive services help residents live independently while remaining connected to their communities.

Eligibility is based on documented disabilities, and properties often provide accommodations to meet residents’ unique needs.


Farmworkers
Farmworkers and their families often face seasonal or year-round housing challenges. Specialized programs offer affordable, safe housing close to agricultural employment, supporting both stability and productivity.

Eligibility requires a substantial portion of income to come from farm labor, and properties may offer additional services to support residents’ needs.
